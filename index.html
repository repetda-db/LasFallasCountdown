History of Las Fallas

🔥 What Are Las Fallas?

Las Fallas (also "Les Falles" in Valencian) is an annual Valencian celebration in honour of Saint Joseph held in Valencia, Spain. The festival takes place every March 15–19 and is one of the world's most spectacular fire festivals, featuring enormous handcrafted papier-mâché sculptures called fallas or ninots, that are ultimately burned on the final night — the Cremà.

📅 Timeline

~18th Century
Carpenters in Valencia began burning wooden waste and old lamp stands (called parots) on the eve of Saint Joseph's Day (March 19), the patron saint of carpenters. These bonfires evolved into elaborate displays with effigies dressed in old clothes.
1784
First documented attempt by Valencia's City Hall to regulate the fallas, requiring permission to erect them and restricting where they could be burned.
1855
Official regulations formalized. Fallas commissions (comissions falleres) began forming as neighbourhood groups that would collectively fund and design each year's monument.
1901
The first official prize for the best falla monument was awarded, turning the festival into a friendly competition between neighbourhoods.
1929
The Fallera Mayor (Festival Queen) tradition was introduced, adding pageantry and cultural ceremony to the event.
1939–1975
Under Franco's regime, the festival was paradoxically both promoted (as a regional tradition) and censored — political satire in the ninots was suppressed.
1990
Las Fallas declared a Festival of International Tourist Interest by the Spanish government, attracting millions of international visitors.
2016
UNESCO inscribes Las Fallas on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity — recognising it as a living cultural tradition of global importance.
Today
Over 750 falla monuments are erected across Valencia each year. The festival attracts more than 1.5 million visitors. The winning ninot is saved from the fire and displayed permanently in the Museu Faller de València.

🎆 Key Events During the Festival

La Plantà (March 15–16): Falla commissions erect their monuments overnight in a frantic 24-hour construction period.

La Mascletà (Daily, 14:00): A deafening daytime firework display in Plaza del Ayuntamiento — pure sound and smoke rather than visual colour.

La Ofrenda de Flores (March 17–18): Thousands of falleros in traditional dress parade through the city to offer flowers to the Virgin Mary, building a giant floral mantle.

La Cremà (March 19, midnight): All monuments — some costing hundreds of thousands of euros — are set alight simultaneously. The city glows orange as years of craft turn to ash in minutes.

🏺 The Ninot Indultat

Each falla features dozens of individual figures called ninots. Before the Cremà, the public votes for their favourite figure from across all fallas. The winner earns the indult — a reprieve from the flames — and is preserved forever in the Museu Faller. This tradition has run since 1934.

🌍 Cultural Significance

Las Fallas is more than a spectacle — it is a communal act of renewal. The burning symbolises the end of winter, the purging of the old, and a joyful welcome of spring. It is deeply tied to Valencian identity, language, and community life. Thousands of Valencians dedicate their entire year — and significant personal savings — to their local falla commission.
